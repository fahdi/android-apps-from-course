2013 Sustained Long-Term Climate Warming Trend

NASA scientists say 2013 tied with 2009 and 2006 for the seventh warmest year since 1880, continuing a long-term trend of rising global temperatures. With the exception of 1998, the 10 warmest years in the 134-year record all have occurred since 2000, with 2010 and 2005 ranking as the warmest years on record. NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies, which analyzes global surface temperatures on an ongoing basis, released an updated report Tuesday on temperatures around the globe in 2013.

GOES Images Central, Eastern U.S. in the Deep Freeze

This Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite GOES) image from Jan. 21 was created at NASA/NOAA's GOES Project at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. GOES data (clouds) were laid down as "primer" over the land and ocean areas, which are derived from Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer or MODIS instrument data. In the image, the colder, higher clouds appear whiter and brighter.

Tracking and Data Relay Satellite To Launch Jan. 23

Launch and mission managers gave a "go" to roll out the Tracking and Data Relay Satellite-L (TDRS-L)/Atlas V stack following the successful completion of today's Launch Readiness Review. The teams also confirmed the launch time of 9:05 p.m. EST on Thursday, Jan. 23 at the opening of a 40-minute launch window. There is an 80% chance of favorable weather. The TDRS-L satellite will be a part of the second of three next-generation spacecraft designed to ensure vital operational continuity for the NASA Space Network.

Opportunity Rover Captures Before-and-After Images

This before-and-after pair of images of the same patch of ground in front of NASA's Mars Exploration Rover Opportunity 13 days apart documents the arrival of a bright rock onto the scene. The rover had completed a short drive just before taking the second image, and one of its wheels likely knocked the rock -- dubbed "Pinnacle Island" -- to this position. The rock is about the size of a doughnut.

NASA Tests Orion Spacecraft Parachute Jettison

Engineers testing the parachute system for NASA's Orion spacecraft increased the complexity of their tests Thursday, Jan. 16, adding the jettison of hardware designed to keep the capsule safe during flight. The test was the first to give engineers in-air data on the performance of the system that jettisons Orion's forward bay cover, a shell that fits over Orion's crew module to protect the spacecraft during launch, orbital flight and re-entry. Orion will be put to its first test in space during Exploration Flight Test-1(EFT-1), in September.
